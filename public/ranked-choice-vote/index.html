How ranked-choice voting works here

  1. 1

    Ask the question, list the options

    Type your question — "Where should we eat?" — and add as many options as you like. No limit on choices and no fixed format.

  2. 2

    Everyone ranks their preferences

    Voters put the options in order — first choice, second choice, and so on — instead of picking just one. Share the link and anyone can vote without signing up.

  3. 3

    See the consensus winner

    Huddlr weighs everyone's rankings and shows the option with the broadest support — the pick that leaves the fewest people unhappy.

Why rank instead of just pick one

  • No spoiler effect. A single-pick poll can crown an option only a third of people wanted. Ranking finds the choice with the widest real support.
  • Fewer re-votes. Because second and third choices count, you usually land on an answer the group accepts the first time around.
  • Free and open to everyone. Share one link; voters rank the options with no account, no download, and nothing to pay.

Ranked-choice voting questions

What is ranked-choice voting, simply?

Instead of choosing one option, each person ranks the options in order of preference. Those rankings are combined to find the choice with the broadest overall support.

Is it good for casual group decisions?

Yes — it's built for everyday calls like where to eat, what to watch, or which name to use, not just formal elections. It's a fast way to settle a group decision fairly.

Can I use it to pick a time instead?

If the decision is really about scheduling, a meeting-time poll or an availability poll will fit better. Use ranked choice when the options aren't dates.
